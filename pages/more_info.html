Definitions

What does it mean to lack digital skills?

For the purpose of this Scorecard, we follow the National Skills Coalition’s use of the term which encompasses those who either have no, or limited, digital skills, as follows:

No digital skills: People with no digital skills failed to meet one or more of the three baseline criteria to even take the full digital skills assessment: 1) prior computer use, 2) willingness to take the computer-based assessment, or 3) ability to complete four out of six very basic computer tasks, such as using a mouse or highlighting text on screen.

Limited digital skills: People with limited digital skills can complete only very simple digital tasks that have a generic interface and just a few steps. As an example, people at this level would have a difficult time sorting email responses to an event invitation into pre-existing folders to keep track of who can and cannot attend an event.

Methods

In this section, we will walk you through an explanation of each indicator, and our criteria for determining whether a state qualifies for a 'yes' or 'no' for each indicator. Each indicator for each state is manually reviewed by our team from publically available information.

The state has identified in-demand digital skills needs using Statewide data.

State has identified in-demand digital skills needs using Statewide data? The State demonstrates a recognition that digital skills are a growing need not only in the technology sector, but across occupations. The strongest States demonstrate this recognition by highlighting specific labor market information combined with an analysis of available data.

State has comprehensive plan to address digital skills needs.

State has comprehensive plan to address digital skills needs? The State has developed a plan that is Statewide, addresses the growing need for digital skills (beyond digital literacy), and that focuses on digital skilling efforts. Comprehensive plans to address digital skills needs go beyond general re-employment efforts (for example, those related to COVID-19 re-employment efforts).

State has a plan to address the digital divide (broadband).

State has a plan for addressing the digital divide (broadband)? The State has officially published specific goals about increasing broadband access and may have published reports focusing on the need for broadband access in the State.

Digital skills are prioritized by the governor.

Digital skills prioritized by governor? The Governor makes an explicit mention to the need to develop digital skills in the State’s workforce during the State of the State speech.

State has innovative digital skills programs at community colleges.

Innovative digital skills programs at community colleges? The State’s community colleges have specific programs or initiatives that target digital skill development, going beyond traditional course offerings in technology-related fields such as Information Technology.

State has technology-related apprenticeship programs planned or in place.

State has technology-related apprenticeship programs planned or in place? The State has either created or demonstrated the intent to create an apprenticeship program that specifically targets digital skills development. The State may also offer apprenticeship programs in growing fields that require digital skills.

Online skilling tools are offered by the State.

Online skilling tool offered by State? The State offers resources that provide online digital skills development or training. The strongest States offer resources beyond a website that includes eligible training provider lists as required under WIOA.

State has additional dedicated digital skills initiatives.

State has additional dedicated digital skills initiatives? The State has other initiatives that may or may not be Statewide and focus on promoting digital skills within the State.

State has dedicated incumbent worker training funds.

The state has dedicated state funding to incumbent worker training.

More Resources

The 2020 National Governor's Association State Guide for Preparing the Future Workforce calls on states to "Create opportunities and provide incentives for state employees to gain technological and digital skills. The state invests in and makes available to all state workers the no-cost advanced training they need for the work of the future."

A paper from Benton highlights efforts around digital equity plan. It highlights several city plans which all have digital skills as one aspect of the plan. Maine appears to be the only state with a plan

The San Francisco digital equity plan includes the goal that "all San Francisco residents have the necessary digital literacy to use technology in the most beneficial ways."

A recent National Skills Coalition paper focuses on digital skills. It includes some ideas about what policy makers could do, such as :

  • Embedding digital literacy and problem-solving skills as allowable or required activities under existing workforce development, adult education, and higher education policies
  • Investing in new Digital Literacy Upskilling grants to expand access to high quality digital skills instruction that meets industry and worker needs. These grants should support the development and implementation of programs that embed digital literacy skills as part of broader occupational skills training, integrated education and training, and other accelerated learning strategies.

This digital skilling policy in the European Union could serve as a helpful model.

The Organization for Economic Co-operation and Development (OECD) has launched a project called Going Digital which aims to "help countries implement an integrated policy approach to the digital transformation".

Some of the key findings in the World Economic Forum's (WEF) 2020 Future of Jobs Report point to the need for upskilling the workforce:

  • The window of opportunity to reskill and upskill workers has become shorter in the newly constrained labour market. This applies to workers who are likely to stay in their roles as well as those who risk losing their roles due to rising recession-related unemployment and can no longer expect to retrain at work. For those workers set to remain in their roles, the share of core skills that will change in the next five years is 40%, and 50% of all employees will need reskilling (up 4%).
  • The public sector needs to provide stronger support for reskilling and upskilling for at-risk or displaced workers. Currently, only 21% of businesses report being able to make use of public funds to support their employees through reskilling and upskilling. The public sector will need to create incentives for investments in the markets and jobs of tomorrow; provide stronger safety nets for displaced workers in the midst of job transitions; and to decisively tackle long-delayed improvements to education and training systems. Additionally, it will be important for governments to consider the longer-term labour market implications of maintaining, withdrawing or partly continuing the strong COVID-19 crisis support they are providing to support wages and maintain jobs in most advanced economies.
